More than a suspicious email

Phishing tries to influence a workplace decision.

Urgency should not replace verification.

Phishing messages often imitate normal workplace communication while adding pressure, secrecy, unfamiliar links or requests for information.

The message may appear to come from a colleague, supplier, customer, delivery service, account platform or senior leader. The goal is often to make the recipient act before reviewing the request carefully.

A safer response does not depend on identifying every possible attack technique. Employees can use a repeatable review process: pause, inspect the details, verify independently and follow the approved reporting route.

Pause

Slow down when a message creates urgency, fear, secrecy or unusual pressure.

Inspect

Review the sender, reply-to address, links, attachments and requested action.

Verify

Confirm the request using a trusted channel that is separate from the original message.

Responding to a suspicious message

Use a clear process instead of guessing.

The appropriate internal process depends on the organization. The steps below provide general awareness guidance and do not replace employer instructions.

Do not forward suspicious content broadly or enter real company information into public tools.

Phishing awareness FAQ

Questions about suspicious messages.

These answers provide general awareness guidance. Company procedures and instructions from appropriate internal teams should guide actual workplace actions.

Ask About Training Options
Can a phishing message look professional?

Yes. A message can use familiar branding, correct spelling and a professional design. Review the full sender details, context, destination and requested action rather than relying only on appearance.

Is every urgent message a phishing attempt?

No. Legitimate messages can be urgent. Urgency is one review signal, especially when it is combined with unusual links, secrecy, credential requests or pressure to bypass normal procedures.

What should I do with an unexpected sign-in link?

Avoid using the link until the message is verified. Open the approved service independently through a known bookmark, company portal or normal account route.

What if the message appears to come from my manager?

Display names and addresses can be misleading. Verify unusual financial, account or information requests through a known separate communication channel.

What if I already clicked the link?

Follow the organization’s incident-reporting and account-support procedures promptly. Provide clear factual information about what happened without attempting unsupported technical remediation.

Does phishing awareness guarantee that attacks will be prevented?

No training or awareness resource can guarantee prevention. Awareness can support more careful decisions when it is combined with appropriate organizational controls, processes and professional security support.
