Requests can arrive through trusted-looking interactions

The request may sound familiar. The identity still needs review.

Verify through a known route rather than through the contact details supplied in the request.

Social-engineering interactions attempt to influence a person’s decision by using trust, pressure, authority, curiosity, helpfulness or concern.

A request may arrive through email, workplace chat, phone, video meeting, text message or an in-person conversation. The person making it may reference real projects, colleagues, suppliers or company procedures.

Employees do not need to determine someone’s intentions immediately. They can pause, review what is being requested and verify the person or process through an approved independent route.

Identity

Confirm who is making the request through a known workplace channel.

Context

Consider whether the request fits the person’s role, project and normal process.

Action

Identify the access, information, money or approval being requested.

“Read the code to me now so I can keep your account from being disabled.”
What needs verification?

  • Whether support would request the code

Request Verification Checklist

  1. Identify who is making the request.
  2. Verify identity through a known independent route.
  3. Confirm that the request fits the work context.
  4. Identify the exact requested action.
  5. Confirm the requester’s authority.
  6. Continue using normal workplace procedures.
  7. Do not let pressure or secrecy replace verification.
  8. Use the approved reporting or support route.

Social-engineering awareness FAQ

Questions about identity and unusual requests.

These answers provide general educational guidance. Employer identity, visitor, access, payment and reporting procedures should guide actual actions.

Ask About Training Options
Does social engineering happen only through email?

No. Requests can arrive through phone calls, workplace chat, text messages, video meetings, social platforms or in-person interactions.

Is caller ID enough to verify a workplace caller?

No. A displayed number or name should not be the only verification method for a sensitive request. Use a known organizational route independently.

What if the requester knows real company details?

Real names, projects, suppliers or public company information do not independently prove identity or authority. Continue using approved verification.

Is it rude to end a call and verify independently?

Verification is a professional control. A legitimate colleague, supplier or support representative should understand that workplace procedures must be followed.

What should I do if I already shared information?

Follow the organization’s reporting or support process promptly. Explain what was shared, through which channel and when the interaction occurred.

Can an in-person visitor present a social-engineering risk?

Yes. Follow visitor identification, escort, access and information-sharing procedures even when the person appears confident or familiar.

Does awareness training prevent every deceptive request?

No awareness resource can guarantee prevention. Training can reinforce verification habits when combined with appropriate controls, procedures and qualified security support.
